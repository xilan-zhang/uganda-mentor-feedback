Founder Profile
Grace Nakamya
Ugandan entrepreneur (from Wakiso District), based in Wakiso District
My name is Grace Nakamya, Ugandan, from here in Wakiso. I have a Diploma. I keep a notebook of prices and customers. I know which farmers in Wakiso grow quality produce and which ones do not.
Business Idea
Fresh Box
I'm thinking about starting a fresh vegetables delivery business. Four years I have been selling tomatoes and onions at Nansana Market. I buy from traders who buy from other traders who buy from farmers. By the time tomatoes reach me, they have passed through three or four hands. A farmer in Wakiso sells at 800 per kilo. I buy at 1,800 or 2,000. I sell at 2,500 or 3,000. By then half the tomatoes are already soft. Some of my regular customers are women who work in offices. Three of them have asked me to bring vegetables to their homes. I did it a few times and they loved it. One of them told her friends and now I think I can get more customers easily through word of mouth. So I am thinking bigger. Go direct to farmers in Wakiso, buy early morning, pack boxes, deliver same day. I will charge 18,000 for a box. Produce will cost me maybe 8,000, transport maybe 3,000. That is 7,000 profit per box. If I deliver 15 boxes per day, that is over 100,000 profit daily. Much better than sitting at the market. My cousin has a boda. He said he can help me with deliveries when he is free. I will buy the vegetables first thing in the morning, pack everything by 9am, then he takes them around while I stay at the market until I have enough delivery customers to leave the market completely. The farmers I know will give me vegetables on credit and I pay them after I collect from customers. That way I do not need much starting capital. I just need to print some flyers and tell people about it. After they taste the freshness, they will keep ordering.

Founder Profile
Esther Nanyonga
Ugandan entrepreneur (from Mukono Town), based in Mukono Town
I am Esther Nanyonga. People call me Essy. I am from Mukono, born here. I did a six-month tailoring course and got my certificate. After that I found work at a tailor shop where I have been for two years now. I can do alterations, repairs, sometimes full garments. Manual machine or electric, both I can use.
Business Idea
Quick Stitch Station
I want to start my own business sewing and repairing school uniforms. I have been working at a tailor shop in Mukono for two years now. Every term it is the same. One week before schools reopen, parents come rushing. Uniforms do not fit, hems need adjusting, buttons are missing, seams torn. They want same-day but my boss tells them come back Thursday, come back next week. Some of them leave angry. Some go looking for another tailor. I keep thinking maybe I could do this myself. I would rent small place near St. Mary's Primary School. Repairs same day if they come early. New uniforms in two days. But I do not know if I am ready. At the shop I work under my boss. She checks my work, she talks to customers, she decides prices. I just sew. Running my own place means doing everything. What if a customer is not happy and I do not know how to handle it? What if I make a mistake and there is no one to fix it? I have one sewing machine, manual. It is old but works. My boss has electric machines that go much faster. During rush time she can finish a hem in five minutes. Mine takes fifteen. If parents want same-day and I am slow, they will go somewhere else. Location is another thing. I found a small room near the school, 60,000 per month. That sounds okay but during holidays when schools are closed, where will money for rent come from? Three months of holiday, that is 180,000 with almost no customers. My younger siblings are still in school. I need to support them. That is why I want to try. But sometimes I think maybe I should just stay at the shop and keep learning. So I am hoping for some advice on this.

Founder Profile
Rebecca Lado
Refugee entrepreneur (from Juba, South Sudan), based in Yumbe District
My name is Rebecca Lado. I am South Sudanese from Juba. I now stay near Bidi Bidi. Before coming here, I got my Diploma in Community Health. Here I work as a volunteer. Every week I visit families, check on pregnant mothers, talk to people about health.
Business Idea
Bidi Bidi Mobile Health Outreach
I hope to start a community health outreach service. I have been a community health volunteer for 5 years around Bidi Bidi. For pregnant mothers, children under five, elderly people with ongoing conditions, I check on them, give advice, refer serious cases to the health center in Yumbe. That is my work. Transport is always the problem. The health center is far and a boda costs 7,000 one way. Most families here do not have that money available when sickness comes. So they delay. An old man with diabetes never gets checked because going to town is too expensive. I have been thinking about this seriously. The health center cannot come to the villages. But perhaps basic services can. A clinical officer travels here every 1-2 months, we set up at a central place, people come for checkups and simple treatments. If we have a small transport fund, we can help the serious cases. I have made some inquiries. Mr. Bosco, a clinical officer at Yumbe Health Center, is willing to participate on his rest days if we can cover his transport, around 30k per visit. The Catholic church in our area has a tent they use for events. Father Michael said we can borrow them if we give notice. I have also asked at the health center about the basic supplies. The district health office requires approval for any health outreach. They want a written proposal with budget, schedule, and list of services. They also want to see that a qualified health worker is involved, not just a volunteer. I have not written this proposal yet but will write it. The funding question is the main challenge. Families here can contribute something small, maybe 1,000 per household per visit. But that will not cover costs. I have identified three NGOs working in this area on health issues, that may be willing to get involved.

Founder Profile
Patrick Mumbere
Refugee entrepreneur (from Goma, DRC), based in Kampala
Patrick Mumbere. I am Congolese, from Goma. I now move to Kampala. I rode boda for four years so I understand this business from inside. I also did a short mechanics course and used to help at a repair shop on Saturdays. When riders around Rubaga want advice on parts, some of them come to me. I have been planning this shop for almost one year, saving money and researching suppliers.
Business Idea
Genuine Parts
I rode boda for four years in Kampala. Every rider I know has lost money to fake spare parts. I bought brake pads once, seller swore they were genuine. Two weeks later they were almost finished. The thing is, genuine parts are expensive and hard to find. You have to go all the way to Kikuubo, spend half the day, and still you are not sure what you are getting. Most riders just buy nearby and hope for the best. I have been researching this for several months. I visited Kikuubo six times, talked to different suppliers. Genuine brake pads from a reliable supplier cost about 15k wholesale. The fake ones cost only half. But in the shops near boda stages, both sell for almost the same price, even higher than the geneuine brake in Kikuubo. So the shops buying fake and selling as genuine are making big money. Riders, like me, are being cheated. My plan is a small shop near the boda stage in Natete. Only genuine parts. Brake pads, chains, mirrors, bulbs, cables. Those things riders need regularly. I will price slightly higher than the fake-sellers but I will guarantee the parts. If something fails within one month, I replace it free. That is my advantage. Riders will pay a bit more for peace of mind. I have calculated the startup. Rent in that area is maybe 150 thousand to 200 thousand per month. Initial stock I estimate 1.2 million to have enough variety. So I need around 1.5 million to start properly. I have saved about 800 thousand from my riding days. I am still figuring out the rest.

Founder Profile
Mary Akello
Refugee entrepreneur (from Juba, South Sudan), based in Ntinda
I am Mary Akello. People in Ntinda know me because I wash and return clothes on time. I came to Uganda from Juba, South Sudan, now living and working in Ntinda doing domestic work and laundry for households. I finished secondary school, and I can read and write. To stay organized, I track customers and pickup days in a small notebook.
Business Idea
CleanHome
This business happened by accident. I was working as house help for a family in Ntinda. The neighbor saw me washing clothes and asked if I could wash for hers too. I said okay. She paid me 15k for the week. Then her friend asked, then someone else. Now I have seven houses paying me every week. I never even looked for these customers. They just came. So I am thinking, why not make this bigger? Ntinda has many families with working parents. They are busy and have money, they do not want to wash their own clothes. If seven customers found me without trying, imagine if I actually go out and find more. I could easily get fifty customers. My plan is to rent a small room in Ntinda. Put a washing machine there. It will be either customers bring clothes, or I collect from their houses. Wash everything with the machine, iron, return in two days. It's much faster than washing by hand. My sister's friend in Kampala does this and she says she makes 400k a month. I can do the same. A washing machine costs about 800k for a good one. I have 200k saved. My brother said he can lend me the rest and I pay him back over six months. With fifty customers paying me 15k each week, I will make over 700k a month and can pay my brother. I am also thinking of quitting my house help job once this grows. That job pays only 150k a month. Laundry business will pay much more. I just need to find the right room with water and electricity. Ntinda has many places so it should not be hard.

Founder Profile
Francis Mugisha
Ugandan entrepreneur (from Kampala), based in Kampala
I'm Francis Mugisha I am Kampala born and raised. I run a small stall near Owino selling phone accessories and doing simple repairs, things I learned by practicing not from any school. I did a short business course that taught me bookkeeping which helps me track what sells and what does not.
Business Idea
Secure Solar Charging
I have been selling phone accessories near Owino Market for three years now, and every day I see the same thing happening to vendors and boda riders around me. Their phones die by midday, and suddenly they cannot do business because mobile money stops working and customers cannot reach them by call. There are people who charge phones in the market, but most vendors I know refuse to use them, because they do not trust leaving their phone with a stranger. What I want to build a charging station where you do not hand your phone to anyone. A cabinet with small compartments, 20 of them, and each one has a door that locks. You come, you put your phone inside and connect it, then you close the door and take the key with you. When you come back in one or two hours, you unlock it, take your phone, pay me maybe 500 or 1,000 depending on time. I think this can work because the trust problem is what stops people from charging. I want to power this with solar because electricity in the market is not reliable and when power goes off the charging stops but sun is always there. I have a small solar panel at my stall already that I use to charge my own phone and run a small light, so I know the basics of how it works. I have about 600k saved so I think I can manage the cabinet and a bigger solar panel and still have something remaining for other expenses.

Founder Profile
Peter Lokiri
Refugee entrepreneur (from Yei, South Sudan), based in Yumbe District
People call me Peter Lokiri. I am from Yei in South Sudan. I have been staying near Bidi Bidi Settlement. My schooling stopped at primary level but I have learned a lot just from doing. I know which tools matter at which time of season.
Business Idea
ToolShare
Here is my idea on tool renting business. Every planting season it is the same story, like farmers running around looking for tools. We share among ourselves but there are not enough tools to go around. Last season I lent my wheelbarrow to a man in the next village. He kept it two weeks. By the time I got it back, I had already missed the best days for moving manure to my garden. I have been thinking about this. What if I bought extra tools and rented them to farmers? Things like wheelbarrows, hoes, pangas, sprayers, these are what people need but cannot afford to buy for themselves. I can charge something small per day. Maybe 1500 for wheelbarrow, 500 for hoe. The idea makes sense to me but I see problems. First one is money to start. A good wheelbarrow costs maybe 80k. Sprayer maybe 150k for a decent one. If I want five wheelbarrows, ten hoes, three sprayers, just to get started. that is already a lot. I would need to borrow from friends and community. Second problem is getting tools back. Farmers here are not bad people, just busy or forgetful. And what if something breaks? If I ask for deposit, most farmers they will pay after harvest, they say. But after harvest is months away. Third problem is everyone needs tools at the same time. When it's planting season, everyone wants wheelbarrow same week. Spraying time, everyone wants sprayer. If I have 3 sprayers and 20 people need them, 17 are disappointed. Then they go borrow from someone else and do not come back to me next time. I have farmed here over ten years. I know these people. They know me. That helps with trust. But trust alone does not make a business work. I need to figure out the money side properly. I see potential with my business idea, before I can actually do it.

Emmanuel Ssekandi
Ugandan entrepreneur (from Kampala), based in Kampala
My name is Emmanuel Ssekandi. I am from here in Kampala. I have a Bachelor's Degree in Arts with a focus on visual design. I taught myself digital design using YouTube tutorials and free software. Now I use Adobe Photoshop, Illustrator, and Canva Pro. I have completed over 400 design projects for clients in Kampala.
Business Idea
DesignSpark Studio
I want to start a digital design studio named DesignSpark, that uses modern technology to help small businesses look professional. I have a bachelor in art, and I have been doing freelance graphic design work for 3 years. I make logos, flyers, social media posts, wedding invitations, business cards. Most of my customers are small shop owners, event planners, and young people starting businesses. They find me through WhatsApp groups and word of mouth. The problem is that everything is slow. A customer sends me a message, I ask what they want, they explain, I make a draft, they say change this, change that, I revise, we go back and forth maybe five or six times. One simple logo can take two weeks because of all the waiting. Sometimes customers disappear in the middle and I have wasted my time. So I am building something different. I want to create a simple app where customers can choose from templates I have designed, customize colors and text themselves, see the result immediately, and pay through mobile money. For customers who want something completely custom, they can still message me directly. But for basic things like social media posts, event flyers, business cards, the app does most of the work. I have already designed over 200 templates. Categories like restaurant menus, salon price lists, church event posters, graduation announcements, shop opening flyers. Things people in Kampala actually need. A customer opens the app, picks a template, types their information, chooses colors, downloads. Five minutes instead of five days. I will charge 5,000 to 15,000 depending on the design. Custom work still 50,000 to 150,000 I found a developer in Ntinda who can build the app for about 2 million shillings. My friend who studied computer science said he can also help me test it and fix bugs.
